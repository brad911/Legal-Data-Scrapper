Mst Basharat Jehan v. Director General Federal Govt. Education, FGEI (C/G) Rawalpindi & others

View PDF

Citation:

2015 SCMR 1418, 2015 SCP 60
Case No. C.A.1184/2011
Jurisdiction: Supreme Court
Category : Dismissal from Service, Policy, Reinstatement, Service Law
Judge: Justice Mushir Alam
11-07-2014
Case Summary: Issues: --Whether the Federal Service Tribunal (FST) erred in dismissing the appellant's appeal challenging her removal from service. --Whether the appellant's removal from service under the Removal of Service (Special Power) Ordinance, 2000, on the ground of misconduct was justified. --Whether the appellant was overaged at the time of her appointment as Assistant Librarian (BPS-09) and subsequent termination from service. --Whether the appellant was entitled to age relaxation as per government policy. ----Decision: The Supreme Court, after considering the arguments and evidence presented, set aside the judgment of the Federal Service Tribunal and allowed the Civil Appeal filed by the appellant. The Court ruled in favor of the appellant, ordering her reinstatement with seniority counted from the date of her appointment letter, but without back benefits for the period she remained out of office. ---Reasoning: The Court based its decision on several grounds: --Age Limit and Relaxation: The Court noted that the age limit for the advertised position of Assistant Librarian (BPS-09) was mentioned as 35 years, with a relaxation of 5 years as per government policy. The appellant, being 37 years old at the time of application, was within the age limit with the relaxation applied. --Procedural Errors: The Court observed that the termination of the appellant's service was based on her alleged overage, despite her appointment being made after fulfilling all codal formalities and providing necessary documents, including proof of age relaxation. --Government Policy: The Court emphasized that the general benefit of age relaxation extended to employees of the Federal Government should not be denied without justifiable reasons, and the respondents failed to show any valid reason for denying such benefit to the appellant. ---Conclusion: The Supreme Court's judgment set aside the decision of the Federal Service Tribunal, ordering the reinstatement of the appellant with seniority from the date of her appointment letter. The case underscores the importance of adherence to government policies and procedural fairness in employment matters.

Baja (decd.) thr. LRs. etc v. Mst. Bakhan, etc

View PDF

Citation:

2015 SCMR 1704, 2015 SCP 65
Case No. C.A.697/2008
Jurisdiction: Supreme Court
Category : Civil Law, Gift, Land/Property Law, Limitation, Permanent Injunction, Suit for Declaration, Validity of Mutation
Judge: Justice Anwar Zaheer Jamali
16-06-2015
Case Summary: Background: The appellants challenged the judgment of the Lahore High Court, which dismissed their civil revision and upheld the decree of the Additional District Judge Nankana Sahib. The decree favored Respondent No. 1 (Mst. Bakhan), an illiterate village woman, in a suit for declaration and permanent injunction regarding ownership and possession of a disputed 9 kanals 1 marla land, which was allegedly fraudulently gifted and subsequently sold to the appellants. Issue: Whether the alleged gift of land by Respondent No. 1 to Respondents No. 2 to 4, and its subsequent sale to the appellants, was valid. Arguments: Appellants: Contended they were bona fide purchasers and argued that the suit was time-barred, having been filed 30 years after the mutation entry of the gift. Respondents: Respondent No. 1, an illiterate and pardanashin lady, claimed she never made the gift and only learned about the fraudulent mutation in 1995, challenging its validity within five months of gaining knowledge. Decision: The Supreme Court dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower courts' judgments that upheld the decree in favor of Respondent No. 1. Reasoning: The Court found no plausible reason supporting the alleged gift, especially considering Respondent No. 1's illiteracy and social vulnerability. Under Article 127 of the Qanun-e-Shahadat Order, 1984, the burden of proving the validity of the gift was on the beneficiaries, but Respondents No. 2 to 4 failed to contest the suit or establish the gift's validity. The appellants, as subsequent buyers, failed to prove the necessary ingredients of a valid gift. The fraudulent nature of the mutation entry rendered it void, and the subsequent sale to the appellants could not be upheld to the extent of the disputed land. The suit was not time-barred as the limitation period began from the date Respondent No. 1 gained knowledge of the fraudulent transaction. Significance: This judgment emphasizes the protection afforded to illiterate and pardanashin women in transactions involving their property rights, especially in cases of alleged gifts. It reaffirms that fraudulent transactions cannot form the basis of subsequent property rights and that the limitation period for challenging such transactions begins upon discovery of the fraud. The Court also clarified that while the decree does not affect the entirety of the sale transaction in favor of the appellants, it excludes the specific land owned by Respondent No. 1.

NLC, a Statutory Organization thr. its Commander v. Irfan Khan and others

View PDF

Citation:

2015 SCMR 1406, 2015 SCP 66
Case No. C.A.1000/2006
Jurisdiction: Supreme Court
Category : Civil Law, Compensation, Damages, Fatal Accident, Vicarious Liability
Judge: Justice Mushir Alam
30-01-2015
Case Summary: ''Where the wrong, damage or injury is caused by two or more persons, each of the wrongdoer is jointly and severally liable to make good the loss to the claimant who suffered at the hands of such tortfeasors. It is the prerogative of the plaintiff to proceed against any or all such wrongdoers. Where a person is injured as a result of negligence on the part of two or more wrongdoers, it is said that the person was injured on account of the composite negligence of those wrongdoers. On the other hand, where a person suffers injury, partly due to his own negligence, then the negligence on the part of the injured which contributed to the accident is referred to as his contributory negligence.'' ---- Issue: Whether the NLC, its driver, and the Karachi Development Authority (KDA), later succeeded by the City District Government Karachi (CDGK), were liable for compensation to the legal heirs of Sher Azam Khan due to the fatal accident. --- Decision: The Supreme Court dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower courts' decisions holding the NLC, its driver, and the CDGK/KDA jointly and severally liable for the accident and compensating the legal heirs of Sher Azam Khan. Reasoning: The Court found that the evidence supported the conclusion of composite negligence involving the NLC driver and the CDGK/KDA. It was established that the NLC driver's rash driving and the CDGK/KDA's failure to take necessary safety measures during road repairs contributed to the fatal accident. The Court emphasized that in cases of composite negligence, each wrongdoer is jointly and severally liable for the entire damages, allowing the victim or their legal heirs to proceed against any or all of the tortfeasors. The NLC's attempt to shift the entire liability to the CDGK/KDA was rejected, as both parties were found to have contributed to the circumstances leading to the accident. Significance: This judgment underscores the legal principle of joint and several liabilities in cases of composite negligence, where multiple parties' negligent acts contribute to a single event causing harm or loss. It reaffirms the responsibility of both vehicle operators and civic authorities to ensure public safety and the potential for joint liability when their combined negligence leads to tragic outcomes.

Lucky Cement Ltd v. Commissioner Income Tax, Peshawar

View PDF

Citation:

2015 SCMR 1494, 2015 SCP 53
Case No. C.A.150/2006
Jurisdiction: Supreme Court
Category : Income Tax, Tax
Judge: JUSTICE MIAN SAQIB NISAR
10-07-2015
Case Summary: Issue: Whether the income received by Lucky Cement from its investments should be taxed as income from business or income from other sources. --- Holding: By a majority of two to one, the appeals were dismissed, and the income was held to be taxable as income from other sources under Section 30(2)(b) of the Income Tax Ordinance, 1979. --- Reasoning: Majority (Justices Sh. Azmat Saeed and Mushir Alam): The income from the investments made by Lucky Cement Ltd. during the construction of its cement plant cannot be considered as income from business under Section 22 of the Income Tax Ordinance, 1979. The investments and the interest earned thereon fall under the category of "Income from other sources" as per Section 30(2)(b) of the Ordinance. The activities of investing surplus funds and earning interest during the construction phase do not constitute business activities within the meaning of the Ordinance. Precedents and the scheme of the Income Tax Ordinance support this interpretation. Dissenting (Justice Mian Saqib Nisar): The income earned from the investments should be considered as business income, as the investments were made as part of the company's business activities, allowed under its Memorandum of Association. --- Precedents Relied Upon: Commissioner of Income Tax v. Liquidator Khulna-Bagerhat Railway Company Ltd (PLD 1962 SC 128) Tuticorin Alkali Chemicals and Fertilizers Ltd. v. Commissioner of Income Tax (1998 PTD 900) Genertech Pakistan Ltd v. Income Tax Appellate Tribunal of Pakistan (2004 SCMR 1319) CIT, Karachi v. Gelcaps (Pvt) Ltd (2009 PTD 331) UCH Power (Pvt) Ltd v. Income Tax Appellate Tribunal (2010 SCMR 1236) --- Facts: Lucky Cement Ltd., a public limited company, invested its surplus funds in various profitable ventures/schemes during the financial years 1994-95, 1995-96, and 1996-97. The income received from these investments was assessed by the Deputy Commissioner Income Tax as income from other sources under Section 30(2)(b) of the repealed Income Tax Ordinance, 1979, differing from the business income rate. The Commissioner on appeal reversed this decision, considering it as business income. This was challenged before the Tribunal, which restored the Assessing Officer's order. The High Court upheld the Tribunal's decision. ---- ''The question is whether income/interest generated from profitable schemes/banks where Public Limited Company (before commencing its business activities) invests its surplus money/reserves amounts to 'income from business' or 'income from other sources. [Per Sh. Azmat Saeed, J.] Main object of the company in the present case as mentioned in its Memorandum of Association was to install, establish and run a cement manufacturing plant. One of the clauses mentioned in the Memorandum of Association provided that company's object and purpose was also "to invest or otherwise deal with the money of the company in such manner as may from time to time be determined". However a prohibitory clause was also mentioned in the Memorandum of Association, which stated in unequivocal terms that in spite of anything contained in any of the object clauses in the Memorandum of Association, nothing therein shall continue to empower the company to undertake or indulge in the business of inter alia investment. Company, in the present case, could, thus, invest its money but such a transaction could not be deemed to be business of the company, consequently, the income derived from such investment must fall in the category of "income from other sources". [Per Mushir Alam, J.] Such activity was carried out during the period when cement plant/factory was under construction, therefore, the company, could not be said to be carrying on any business at that point of time within the contemplation of S. 22 of the Income Tax Ordinance, 1979. Such income was rightly assessed as 'income from other sources" under S. 30(2)(b) of the Income Tax Ordinance, 1979.''

Case Summary: Issues Presented: Whether a work-charge employee with over 14 years of continuous service in a role of a permanent nature is entitled to regularization of service under the Industrial and Commercial Employment (Standing Orders) Ordinance, 1968, and consistent with the principles of equality and non-discrimination enshrined in Articles 25 and 199 of the Constitution of Pakistan. --- Decision: The Supreme Court dismissed the petition filed by the Province of Punjab, upheld the High Court's judgment favoring regularization of the respondent's services, and imposed a cost of Rs.50,000 on the petitioners. ---Reasoning: The Court reasoned that the continuous and long-term nature of the respondent's employment as a Storekeeper, a role inherently permanent, entitles him to regularization under the Industrial and Commercial Employment (Standing Orders) Ordinance, 1968. This ordinance defines a "permanent workman" as one engaged in work of a permanent nature for more than nine months. The Court distinguished this case from Province of Punjab v. Gul Hassan (1992 PLC 924), where work-charged employees were not deemed permanent due to specific statutory rules. The Court also referenced Punjab Seed Corporation v. The Court's decision is grounded in the principles of fairness, equality, and non-discrimination under Articles 25 and 199 of the Constitution. --- Rule of Law: Work-charge employees who have continuously served in roles of a permanent nature for an extended period are entitled to regularization of their services under the relevant labor laws and the Constitution of Pakistan, particularly in light of Articles 25 (equality before law) and 199 (jurisdiction of High Courts). ---- Significance: This landmark judgment reaffirms the Supreme Court's commitment to upholding the rights of long-serving work-charge employees to be treated equitably and to have their services regularized. It emphasizes the application of labor laws and constitutional principles to ensure justice and fairness in employment practices within Pakistan. ---- Precedents Cited: Province of Punjab v. Case Summary: Issue: Whether the absence of a written instrument regarding the creation of tenancy is sufficient to negate the existence of a landlord-tenant relationship between the parties, particularly in the context of the West Pakistan Urban Rent Restriction Ordinance (VI of 1959) and the Constitution of Pakistan (1973), Article 199. Holding: The Supreme Court reversed the High Court's decision, holding that a landlord need not necessarily be the owner of the property and that tenancy can be established through means other than a written instrument. The Court reinstated the ejectment order. Reasoning: The Supreme Court reasoned that the title of the property, while not disputed, coupled with the absence of rebuttal evidence, strongly presumes the existence of a tenancy relationship. The Court emphasized that tenancy can be oral and implied, and the respondent failed to substantiate his claim of possession as a J&K refugee with evidence. The Supreme Court found no legal or factual infirmities in the concurrent findings of the subordinate courts regarding the respondent's status as a tenant and criticized the High Court's overreach in its writ jurisdiction. Cited Precedents and Statutes: West Pakistan Urban Rent Restriction Ordinance (VI of 1959), Sections 2(c) & 13 Constitution of Pakistan (1973), Article 199 Conclusion: The Supreme Court set aside the judgment of the Lahore High Court, allowing the appeal and reinstating the ejectment order, with no order as to costs.
